Ada Lovelace: The First Coder

In 1843, mathematician Ada Lovelace wrote the first computer algorithm—a century before digital computers existed. Her visionary notes on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine laid the foundation for modern programming, the tech in your pocket, and the algorithms that power AI today.

Rosalind Franklin: Photo 51

British crystallographer Rosalind Franklin captured Photo 51—the X-ray diffraction image that revealed DNA's double helix structure. Her experimental evidence transformed molecular biology and saved countless lives through genetic medicine, yet her legacy remained hidden for decades.

Hedy Lamarr: The Inventor Actress

Hollywood actress Hedy Lamarr developed frequency-hopping spread spectrum technology during WWII to prevent torpedo jamming. Her military innovation became the invisible foundation for modern Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and cellular communications—the wireless technology connecting billions today.

Nellie Bly: Journalism’s STEM Pioneer

Nellie Bly revolutionized investigative journalism with her undercover reporting and record-breaking trip around the world. She also invented a practical steel drum for the oil industry, proving that innovation in STEM and social reform can come from unexpected places.

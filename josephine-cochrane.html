Josephine Cochrane: The Woman Who Invented the Dishwasher

Published on January 22, 2026

Introduction

Josephine Garis Cochrane was an American inventor whose determination and ingenuity revolutionized everyday life by creating the first commercially successful dishwasher. At a time when household chores like washing dishes were tedious and time-consuming, Cochrane’s invention applied water pressure to clean dishes — a method that became the foundation for modern dishwashers. Although other early designs existed, none were as effective or practical as hers, and her work paved the way for the widespread adoption of dishwashing machines in kitchens around the world. Cochrane not only invented a device that eased domestic labour but also broke barriers as a female inventor and business owner in the late 19th century.

Early Life

Josephine Garis was born on March 8, 1839, in Ashtabula County, Ohio, and raised in Valparaiso, Indiana, in a family that valued education and innovation. Her father, John Garis, was a civil engineer, and although she did not receive formal training in mechanics or engineering, she grew up around discussions about machines and tools. In 1858, she married William A. Cochran, a prosperous dry goods merchant, and the couple eventually settled into a comfortable life in Shelbyville, Illinois. They entertained frequently with fine china, but Cochrane grew frustrated when her servants chipped the dishes while washing them. This irritation planted the early seeds of her inventive mind.

Her Work

After William Cochran’s death in 1883, Josephine found herself with significant debts and few prospects, so she turned her energy toward solving the problem she knew well: dishwashing. In the shed behind her house, she began experimenting with a machine that could wash dishes using the force of water rather than manual scrubbing. She measured cups, plates, and saucers carefully and designed custom wire compartments to hold each piece securely. These racks sat inside a large copper boiler. A motor turned a wheel beneath the rack, and hot soapy water was forced upward, cleaning the dishes thoroughly. This design was the first to use water pressure effectively and consistently, rather than scrubbers, which made it far more efficient.

Cochrane filed for her first patent on December 28, 1886, and founded the Garis-Cochran Manufacturing Company to build and sell her machines. She personally marketed and demonstrated the dishwasher to potential customers, though at first most demand came from restaurants and hotels, not households, because many homes lacked sufficient hot water or the machines were still expensive. Her dishwasher was showcased at the 1893 World’s Columbian Exposition in Chicago, where it won an award for its mechanical construction, durability, and practical design.

Why History Often Overlooked Her

Despite her significant contribution, Josephine Cochrane did not receive widespread recognition during her lifetime. The late 19th and early 20th centuries were dominated by male inventors and business owners, and women’s achievements in mechanics were often downplayed or ignored. Cochrane faced skepticism from male mechanics who doubted her understanding of mechanical work, and she had to fight to have her design built according to her ideas rather than theirs. Even her public persona sometimes emphasized stories that made her seem more like a wealthy socialite seeking convenience than a serious inventor — a narrative that potentially obscured her true ingenuity. It wasn’t until much later that her role in developing modern dishwashers was fully appreciated.

Legacy

Josephine Cochrane died in Chicago on August 3, 1913, at the age of 74. Her company continued after her death, eventually becoming part of what is now KitchenAid, a division of the Whirlpool Corporation. While dishwashers did not become a common household appliance until the mid-20th century — when homes had more reliable hot water and improved detergents — Cochrane’s original innovation laid the groundwork for the machines we use today. In 2006, she was posthumously inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame, honoring her influential patent and her impact on daily life.
